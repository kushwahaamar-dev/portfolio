I’m a computer-science student in the Honors College at Texas Tech. Two things have my attention: AI agents that have to prove they behaved, and how a seizure spreads from one brain region to the next. The agent side has turned into real things — a secret scanner you install from npm, contracts that slash an agent’s stake when it breaks its word, a payment stream that pays an AI only for work it can prove. The brain side runs the kind of model used to plan epilepsy surgery, about a thousand times faster.

Now June 2026 — Applied Scientist Intern at Tempora Labs this summer, on agentic AI. The rest of my time goes to the epilepsy lab, where we’re testing whether cognitive effort can quiet a seizure before it spreads.

NeuroFormer research-grade Python library

An EEG library that classifies psychiatric conditions from raw recordings — a graph network for which electrodes fire together, a transformer for how the signal moves over time. A big jump over the 38.5% GNN+CNN baseline it was built to beat. Pip-installable, with self-supervised pretraining and ONNX export.

Universal Sentinel GDACS · NASA · NOAA

Watches live disaster feeds — GDACS, NASA, NOAA — every 60 seconds and asks an AI one question per event: release aid, yes or no? A yes pays a pre-verified NGO from an on-chain vault; anything ambiguous defaults to no, and only the AI’s own wallet can move the money.

Research

Epileptic brain networks Texas Tech · Honors research

Simulating how a seizure spreads region to region across a brain — the kind of model used to test an epilepsy surgery before the operation. I rewrote the simulation loop with Numba, which made runs about 1000× faster and let me scale from 76 up to 998 brain regions. It feeds the lab’s work on whether cognitive effort can suppress a seizure.

source
RAWRB Accepted to the ACL 2026 Student Research Workshop

A benchmark for whether a small model’s step-by-step reasoning actually drives its answer or just rationalizes one it already had. The uncomfortable finding: bigger models got more accurate but less faithful, and only 4–14% of answers were both correct and honestly reached.
